# Competitor Teardown

## Method note

Direct fetches blocked by network policy (see `01-current-site-audit.md`).
This teardown combines:

1. WebSearch snippets and indexed page descriptions for each site
2. Public design-press write-ups (SCA Coffee Design Awards, Sprudge,
   Daily Coffee News, Sample Coffee transparency legend)
3. Cross-referenced brand asset libraries (Brandfetch) and case studies

Where a pattern is sourced from a write-up rather than the live site,
I've kept the description concrete rather than vague. Anything I'm
inferring from category convention rather than evidence is marked
`[INFERRED]`.

## Per-site reads

### Caravela Coffee — `caravela.coffee`

**Positioning.** "Latin American green coffee exporter and importer."
Almost 4,000 producers across 8 Latin American countries; ~90% of staff
based at origin (agronomists, cuppers). The closest structural parallel
to Discovery — vertically integrated, origin-controlled trader serving
roasters.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Transparency reports as a product.** Lot-level reports show which
  producers contributed parchment to each exported lot and what they
  were paid. Discovery should mirror this for coffee, vanilla, cocoa.
- **Community lots vs microlots framing.** Two-tier traceability story
  — bulk-but-traceable community lots; named-producer microlots at a
  premium. Same pattern works for cocoa (community ferment lot vs
  cooperative single-origin) and vanilla (region blend vs single curer).
- **"X% of our team is at origin" stat.** Reframes the company from
  trader to operator. Discovery owns the Namanve facility — make that
  the equivalent stat.
- **EUDR-compliance content as marketing.** Caravela publishes a
  dedicated EUDR explainer. For European buyers in 2026 this is a
  qualifier; absence is a red flag.

### Trabocca — `trabocca.com`

**Positioning.** Dutch importer, Ethiopia at the core, "tree to cup."
Most relevant African-origin parallel.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Origin pages as long-form editorial.** Each country (Ethiopia,
  Rwanda, etc.) gets a dedicated long page combining geography,
  varieties, processing, history. Discovery should do this for Uganda
  itself — one anchor page that owns "Uganda" as an origin story.
- **"Pursuit: Impact & Sourcing Report"** — single annual deliverable
  doubling as both ESG report and design asset. Discovery's
  sustainability page should aspire to this rather than a brochure
  paragraph.
- **Blockchain traceability angle.** Trabocca partnered with Fairfood
  on TraceDaily. Discovery doesn't need blockchain itself — but a
  publicly addressable lot ID per shipment is the design pattern
  worth borrowing.

### Falcon Coffees — `falconcoffees.com`

**Positioning.** B2B importer serving ~600 roasters in UK/Europe + a
North American arm in Austin.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Clean B2B portal mindset.** Customer login for stock and lot flow.
  Discovery doesn't need a login portal at MVP, but the inquiry form
  should feel like the front door of a B2B operation, not a brochure
  contact form.
- **"Services" page** that explicitly lists what a roaster gets
  (sampling, logistics, financing, quality) — reframes the company
  from "we sell coffee" to "we run your origin supply chain."

### Sucafina — `sucafina.com` / `group.sucafina.com`

**Positioning.** Global trader, Geneva-founded 1977, 950 staff in 28
countries. "Farm to Roaster" vision.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Programmatic farmer-impact framing.** Each social initiative gets
  a one-line headline + an outcome ("rebuilt a clinic," "fund a
  technical college," "distribute saplings"). Concrete and outcome-led,
  not abstract values copy. Discovery should narrate its farmer
  relationships the same way — one sentence, one outcome, no vague
  "empowerment" language.
- **Regional sub-sites** (`sucafina.com/emea`, `/na`) without losing
  brand unity. Not relevant at Discovery's scale, but the pattern
  validates the corporate-vs-Orocafe split we're planning.

### Belco — `belco.fr`

**Positioning.** French importer (Mérignac), founded 2014, 71 staff,
11 Q-graders, 93% of coffee direct from producers, sub-brands like
*Café de Forêt©*, *Fresh Coffee Clean Ocean©*, *Soil of Excellence©*,
*Impact Score©*.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Producer profile template — gold standard.** Each producer has a
  consistent layout: name, country region, elevation, farm size,
  varieties, processing, photo, quote. Discovery's redesign needs
  exactly this template for Ugandan producers and cooperatives.
- **Named impact programmes.** Rather than a generic "sustainability"
  page, Belco runs named, ®-branded initiatives. Discovery could
  follow with one or two named programmes (e.g. an Orocafe roasted-at-
  origin programme, a specific cooperative partnership name) — but
  only if real; do not invent.
- **"How do we buy our products?" sourcing page** — explains the
  buying model. Discovery should publish its equivalent: how lots are
  sourced, sampled, processed, exported.

### La Cabra — `lacabra.com` (formerly `lacabra.dk`)

**Positioning.** Aarhus specialty coffee roaster (2012). Editorial
benchmark.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **"Bloom" photography series.** La Cabra runs a longform travel-
  photography editorial parallel to its commerce site. This is the
  template for Discovery's photography brief — pictures should be
  documentary, not packshot.
- **Nordic typography sensibility** — serif headlines, generous
  leading, restrained UI. For Orocafe specifically.
- **Café locations as separate pages with their own visual identity**
  — validates a slightly different visual treatment for distinct
  brand expressions under one parent (analogous to Discovery /
  Orocafe split).

### Onyx Coffee Lab — `onyxcoffeelab.com`

**Positioning.** Specialty roaster, Arkansas. Industry-cited gold
standard for product-page transparency.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Per-product transparency strip.** On every coffee product page:
  FOB price, cupping score, amount purchased, social premiums paid,
  the commodity-coffee price and Fair Trade price *at time of
  purchase*, and the procurement route (direct trade vs importer).
  This is the highest-density transparency design in the category.
- **Score as headline element.** Cupping score is treated as a primary
  on-card data point, not buried.
- **For Discovery:** every Orocafe product card should publish FOB
  paid to producer, cup score, elevation, processing, harvest year
  — and the B2B commodity card should publish the equivalent
  traceability strip (origin district, cooperative, processing, lot
  ID, certification, harvest year).

### Sey Coffee — `seycoffee.com`

**Positioning.** Brooklyn roaster. Restrained-premium benchmark.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Discipline of restraint.** Soft palette, generous whitespace, no
  visual filler. Validates the "magazine-grade" requirement in the
  brief: cut anything that isn't doing work.
- **Detailed coffee pages with flavor notes + origin info** treated
  as the *only* content on the page — no upsell, no module sprawl.
- **For Orocafe specifically:** Sey is the closest tonal target. La
  Cabra is the editorial layer on top.

### The Coffee Collective — `coffeecollective.dk`

**Positioning.** Copenhagen direct-trade roaster, B Corp. The
"transparency as content" exemplar named in the brief.

**What's worth stealing:**
- **Annual transparency report** at its own subdomain
  (`transparency.coffeecollective.dk`) — a destination, not a
  footer-buried PDF.
- **Quality Bonus on the bag.** They print the exact additional
  payment to the producer above market price on the physical product.
  Brand-defining gesture. The digital equivalent: print the lot-level
  premium on the product page.
- **Signed the Transparency Pledge (2019)** — and they say so. If
  Discovery joins any equivalent industry initiative, surface it the
  same way.

## Synthesized patterns

### Typography

- **Editorial serif headlines + clean grotesque body** is the dominant
  combo across La Cabra, Sey, Coffee Collective. (Onyx and Sucafina
  lean more contemporary sans-only; Caravela mixes.)
- **Generous leading** (1.5–1.7) on body, **tight tracking on
  display** — magazine convention, not web-app convention.
- **Headline scale is large.** 72px+ display sizes on desktop hero
  are normal for this category. Don't be timid.
- For Discovery (corporate): a confident serif display + neutral sans
  body. For Orocafe: same family system, but lighter weights and
  more whitespace to read editorial / consumer-premium.

### Photography

- **Origin documentary photography**, not product-on-white. People,
  hands, drying beds, washing stations, landscape. Wide and mid
  shots dominate; close-ups of cherries/beans punctuate.
- **No gradient overlays.** When type sits on photo, it's either in
  a clear empty area of the frame or photo and type are separated
  by layout, not by overlay.
- **Square / 4:5 portrait crops** for producer profiles are the
  category convention (Caravela, Belco). 16:9 only for landscape.
- **Black-and-white** appears occasionally for stylized editorial
  moments (Sey, La Cabra) — useful for restraint when colour
  origin photos start to feel busy.

### Information architecture

- **Origin-first navigation.** Top-level nav often leads with
  "Origins" or "Coffees" rather than "Products." Discovery's nav
  should put commodities, traceability, and Orocafe up front; "About"
  is secondary.
- **Origin pages are long.** 2000+ words plus 6–12 images is normal.
  Single-screen brochure pages are not.
- **Sustainability / impact is a destination, not a tab in About.**
  Trabocca's Pursuit, Sucafina's farmer programs, Coffee Collective's
  transparency report — all standalone.
- **Producer / cooperative profiles get their own URLs.** Repeating
  template; one URL per producer. Discovery should plan this even if
  it launches with 3–5 profiles.

### Transparency content

Best-in-class transparency disclosure (synthesised from Onyx, Caravela,
Coffee Collective):

| Field | On product card? | On product page? |
|---|---|---|
| Origin region / district | yes | yes |
| Cooperative / producer name | when available | yes |
| Elevation (m.a.s.l.) | optional | yes |
| Processing method | yes | yes |
| Variety (Arabica) / type (Robusta SC15/18) | yes | yes |
| Harvest year | yes | yes |
| Cup score (Q-grade) | yes | yes |
| FOB price paid | optional | yes |
| Premium above commodity / FT | no | yes |
| Certifications | yes (icons) | yes (named) |
| Lot ID | optional | yes |

For Discovery's B2B commodity cards: use the same skeleton, but for
commodities where Q-grading doesn't apply (vanilla, cocoa, chilli),
substitute the category-appropriate quality metric (vanillin %,
fermentation grade, capsaicin / SHU).

### Product presentation

- **Commerce cards in this category are quiet.** No "SALE!" badges,
  no urgency timers, no review-star clutter. The card communicates
  *quality of object*, not *deal*.
- **Cards differ between B2B and consumer.** B2B cards lead with
  specs (origin / process / certification / MOQ). Consumer cards
  lead with photography and a flavour-note line.
- **Hover states are subtle.** Image swap or slight elevation; not
  zoom-in or overlay.
- **Filtering** on origin/process/certification is expected on the
  index page once there are >6 products. Build for it from day one.

## Direct application to Discovery

The competitor read converges on a clear instruction set:

1. **Lead the home page with origin documentary photography**, not
   product. (Caravela, Trabocca, La Cabra.)
2. **Publish a transparency strip on every product**, B2B and
   consumer. (Onyx is the template.)
3. **Build a `/producers` (or `/cooperatives`) section with a
   repeating profile template.** (Belco is the template.)
4. **Treat sustainability as a destination page**, not a footer.
   (Trabocca's Pursuit report is the model.)
5. **Two-track visual system: corporate (Discovery) vs editorial /
   premium (Orocafe).** Same family, different weight & whitespace.
   (La Cabra and Sey for tonal references on the Orocafe side;
   Caravela / Belco / Sucafina on the corporate side.)
6. **Use the Namanve facility as a positioning asset.** Most peers
   are buying coffee from a country; Discovery operates *in* the
   country and *processes at* origin. The site has to look that way.
